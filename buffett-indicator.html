Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Buffett Indicator?

The Buffett Indicator is the ratio of total US stock market capitalization to GDP. Warren Buffett called it "probably the best single measure of where valuations stand at any given moment." When this ratio is high, stocks may be overvalued; when low, they may be undervalued.

What do the levels mean?

Below 75%: Significantly undervalued — historically great buying opportunity. 75-100%: Fair value range. 100-140%: Modestly overvalued — caution advised. Above 180%: Extremely overvalued — dot-com bubble peaked at ~190%.

How is this calculated?

Buffett Indicator = (Total US Stock Market Cap / US GDP) × 100. We estimate total market cap from the S&P 500 index using historical Wilshire 5000 correlation. GDP data is from the Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA).

Should I invest based on this?

The Buffett Indicator is one signal among many. It's useful for understanding overall market sentiment but shouldn't be the sole basis for investment decisions.
